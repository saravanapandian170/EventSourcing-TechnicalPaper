# Grit and Growth Mindset – Answers

---

## **1**
- Angela Lee Duckworth found that **IQ is not enough** to predict student success.  
- She discovered that **grit**—long-term passion and perseverance—is the strongest predictor of achievement.  
- Grit means having **stamina** and working toward goals for years, not just short periods.  
- It is not strongly linked to **natural talent** and can even be opposite to it.  
- Scientists still don’t fully know how to build grit, but promoting a **growth mindset** is the most promising approach.  

---

## **2**
- The video explains Carol Dweck’s idea of the **growth mindset**, which helps people learn and achieve more.  
- A growth mindset means believing that **skills and intelligence can improve** with effort and practice.  
- A fixed mindset means thinking abilities are **permanent** and cannot change.  
- People with a growth mindset see **challenges, mistakes, and feedback** as chances to get better.  
- Because mindset strongly affects success, many **schools, teams, and companies** use this approach to build better learning habits.  

---

## **3**
### **Internal Locus of Control**
- Believing that **your own actions, effort, and decisions** shape your results.  
- People with an internal locus feel they can influence outcomes through **hard work and choices**.

### **Key Point in the Video**
- People stay motivated longer when they believe they **control their outcomes**, not luck or external factors.  
- Focusing on **effort** leads to better performance, motivation, and handling challenges.  
- The main message: **building an internal locus of control increases motivation and success**.  

---

## **4**
- Believe that you **can figure things out**  
- **Challenge** your negative assumptions  
- Create and follow your own **life curriculum** for learning  
- **Honor the struggle** instead of avoiding it  
- Treat **setbacks** as part of the process and stay persistent  

---

## **5**
- Stay with a problem until you solve it; **don’t quit halfway**  
- See confusion, discomfort, and errors as **signals to learn**, not problems  
- Don’t write code you don’t understand; aim for **clear, readable, modular** solutions  
- Take full **responsibility** for your learning and projects  
- Build your own **learning routine** (Documentation → Google → StackOverflow → Ask)  
- Remember that skills grow with **time, effort, and consistency**
